# 'Delete all traffic on this': what the Mandelson emails reveal

**Declassified government documents show Britain's Washington ambassador
operated as an extension of No.10's political machine — bypassing normal
diplomatic channels, criticising his own government's budget to a senior
civil servant, and taking part in an instruction to delete sensitive
communications during a live parliamentary inquiry.**

---

Three days after Christmas 2024, officials at the Foreign, Commonwealth and
Development Office were working over the holiday period on an urgent problem.
Peter Mandelson — just appointed UK Ambassador to Washington — had committed to
a paid panel appearance at UBS's Greater China Conference in Shanghai, scheduled
for 11–13 January, the same week he was due to start as His Majesty's Ambassador
to the United States. He had deliberately timed his start date to leave himself
free to honour the engagement.

The FCDO consulted five separate teams simultaneously: the British Embassy
Washington, the China Desk, the Americas Directorate, Human Rights and Democracy,
and Security. The decision was kicked upstairs to No.10's special advisers. "As he
will not by then have taken up employment with HMG," one official wrote, "it is a
matter for LPM whether or not he proceeds with this event. He has asked that any
concerns are flagged to SpAds so that they and No 10 can take a view as to whether
they wish to intervene."

The episode — one of hundreds captured in a 545-page release of internal
government documents published under a Humble Address to Parliament — sets the
tone for what follows. The documents, spanning December 2024 to September 2025,
reveal an ambassadorial appointment unlike any in recent British diplomatic
history: a senior political figure who communicated primarily through an informal
network rooted in No.10's inner circle, who submitted advice to the Prime Minister
uninvited, who asked a colleague to delete sensitive messages during an active
parliamentary inquiry, and who told the Treasury's most senior civil servant that
his own government's budget was a political mistake.

---

**The security clearance that nobody planned for**

The problems began before the appointment was even finalised. In the final days
of December 2024, officials at FCDO wrote to the Cabinet Office with a basic
question: did Mandelson — as a member of the House of Lords — need Developed
Vetting clearance at all?

Cabinet Office initially said no: existing exemptions for parliamentarians would
apply. Then, over the Christmas weekend, they reversed themselves. The exemptions
policy, they concluded, "had not envisaged the unusual situation of MPs/Lords being
appointed to Ambassadorial positions." A fast-tracked DV was completed by 29
January 2025.

Part of the urgency was American. Officials noted that "the US are strict about
clearance matters, and will likely check Lord Mandelson's clearance with ESND ahead
of any sensitive discussions." Mandelson's own response to the vetting questionnaire
acknowledged the scope of the problem. Asked to identify foreign contacts, he
replied: "I have a large number of personal acquaintances with foreign nationals but
I would not describe these as current friendships or personal business connections."
The standard questionnaire, designed for civil servants, was simply not built for
the network assembled over a career that included founding Global Counsel, one of
Westminster's most connected lobbying firms.

---

**'Delete all traffic on this'**

The most striking passage in the documents dates from 13 March 2025. Mandelson
forwarded a WhatsApp exchange he had just had with Karen Pierce — the outgoing
Washington Ambassador — directly to Morgan McSweeney, Keir Starmer's chief of
staff. The message included an explicit instruction:

"I have just had this exchange with *** by whatsapp. I don't know why Karen decided
to email everyone last night... Please ignore — and if you can do it discreetly,
delete!"

Within hours, Ailsa Terry — No.10's senior foreign affairs official — confirmed the
instruction had been relayed to the top of FCDO. "Olly has been clear about the need
to delete all traffic on this," she wrote.

"Olly" is Sir Oliver Robbins, FCDO Permanent Under-Secretary. Six months later,
with the Foreign Affairs Committee pressing FCDO over Mandelson's appointment,
Robbins wrote to colleagues in terms that cast this earlier episode in a different
light. "I will speak to Dan about making sure we are keeping the creation of new
disclosable evidence within reasonable bounds," he wrote, in a message marked
OFFICIAL-SENSITIVE.

The two documents — an instruction to delete in March, a note about limiting new
evidence in September — sit uncomfortably alongside each other, though FCDO has
not commented on either.

---

**The political machine in Washington**

What the documents most clearly show is the shape of the network through which
Mandelson actually operated. His top five contacts were not the Foreign Secretary,
the FCDO Permanent Secretary's team, or career diplomats. They were Ailsa Terry
(No.10 foreign affairs, 26 documents), Oliver Robbins (23), Jonathan Powell —
Starmer's National Security Adviser (16), Morgan McSweeney (14), and Michael Ellam,
Second Permanent Secretary at Cabinet Office (9).

The McSweeney channel in particular reads less like ambassador-to-Whitehall
reporting and more like a political operator staying in close contact with his
principal. Mandelson sent McSweeney informal intelligence briefings from private
dinners — including one with Trump's close associate Mark Burnett, copied to no
FCDO official. He circulated a sensitive economic policy document for the UK-US
summit with the instruction "please don't forward." He forwarded the Karen Pierce
messages with the deletion request. And on 1 April 2025, he dropped McSweeney a
casual note that began: "I just was in WH seeing the VP about something else."

A meeting with the Vice President of the United States, disclosed as a passing aside.

---

**The Powell relationship**

Jonathan Powell, who served as Tony Blair's chief of staff before taking the
National Security Adviser role under Starmer, features in 43 documents. The
relationship is a peer one. Powell wrote to Mandelson in February 2025 about
preparation for the PM's Washington summit: "When I see *** tomorrow we will give
him the suggestion for what we could announce on the economic relationship when Keir
visits — will send the draft to you this evening for comment."

Mandelson's reply asked for a readout of the SMG — the Senior Ministers Group on
the US, a restricted decision-making body. His expectation of access to its
conclusions places him inside the most senior No.10 deliberations on the
Washington relationship, not as a briefed subordinate but as a participant.

In April, after a request from Mandelson on an entirely redacted subject, Powell
confirmed the result in one line: "Yes we are making sure that happens. Matt Collins
and his team are doing it." Matt Collins is the Deputy National Security Adviser.
The NSC machinery was responding to the Ambassador's ask.

---

**'Counter to the pre-election narrative'**

The Whitehall tour that Mandelson conducted in January 2025 — visiting a sequence
of Cabinet ministers before departing for Washington — produced a remarkable
exchange with the Treasury. In a meeting with James Bowler, Permanent Secretary to
HM Treasury, Mandelson offered an assessment of the government's October 2024
Budget that went well beyond diplomatic small talk.

"LM said markets, businesses and investors gave the Government significant goodwill
on entering office, but he felt they lacked a clearly articulated, deep-seated
'growth philosophy'," the readout records. "He thought the business community had
not been expecting what was in the Budget in the autumn. He accepted the fiscal
correction was necessary, but this had come at the expense of business and was
counter to the pre-election narrative."

Bowler's reply was procedural. Covid and energy interventions had forced difficult
choices, he said. The record stands regardless: the incoming Ambassador used a
bilateral with the most senior civil servant at HM Treasury to deliver a political
critique of the Chancellor before he had left for Washington.

---

**The China question**

The documents revisit Mandelson's China connections throughout. In September 2024,
months before his appointment was announced, he was receiving briefings from FCDO's
China Department ahead of a speaking engagement. His reply to the draft lines he
was given is revealing: "It has to be turned into something I can say! The drift is
fine. It mustn't sound naive ie we are vigilant and have eyes wide open but we also
want a serious sustained relationship."

He was not receiving the official line — he was editing it. By December 2024, the
Christmas-period scramble over the UBS Shanghai conference had made clear that his
China associations were an active management problem for officials, not a settled
matter. The Q&A prepared for his appointment announcement flagged the issue
explicitly: "I know we need more on his background (links with China etc)."

As Ambassador, Mandelson's own written communications project a markedly
different tone. In a March 2025 scenesetter to Business Secretary Jonathan
Reynolds, he wrote that the American trade agenda aimed to create "a new global
trade order with allies working closely together (following US rules) that leaves
China increasingly on the 'other side of the fence'." The language echoes America
First policy far more than standard FCO positioning.

---

**What the documents show**

Ambassadors are not, in general, supposed to operate as political proxies for the
prime minister's office. They report to the Foreign Secretary. They communicate
through official channels. They receive clearances before sensitive discussions, not
after. They do not instruct colleagues to delete WhatsApp exchanges.

What these documents show is an appointment that was unusual in almost every
dimension: the security clearance was improvised in real time; the China
associations required emergency consultation over Christmas; the Whitehall tour
produced frank political commentary for official records; and the operating network
in post ran almost entirely through No.10 rather than the diplomatic machine.

The Foreign Affairs Committee was asking questions about this appointment as
recently as September 2025. Sir Oliver Robbins was, by then, concerned about the
creation of new disclosable evidence. The documents that have been disclosed are
extensive. It is not clear what has not been.
